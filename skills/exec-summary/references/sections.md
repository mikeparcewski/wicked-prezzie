# Executive Summary — 8-Section Schema

Each section below defines the required structure for one part of the executive
summary. Every section must contain substantive content before the summary can
be approved. The exec summary is a prose document — not slides, not bullet
points, not an outline.

---

## Section 1 — Executive Framing

**Purpose**: Set the stage. Define the problem in the client's terms, establish
urgency, and describe what success looks like.

**Required content**:
- Current-state problem statement (in the client's language, referencing their
  named systems, teams, or processes)
- Business drivers and constraints (what forces are creating the need — contract
  expiry, growth, regulatory change, technology debt)
- "Why now" urgency (what happens if the client does nothing, or delays by 12
  months)
- Success definition (what the world looks like when this is done well — stated
  in measurable terms where possible)

**Quality criteria**:
- Problem statement references at least one named client system or process
- Business drivers are specific (not "digital transformation" — name the actual
  driver)
- Urgency is grounded in a timeline, event, or consequence — not vague FUD
- Success definition includes at least one measurable outcome

**Prohibited patterns**:
- "TBD", "TODO", "to be determined", "placeholder"
- Generic industry problems not specific to this client
- Urgency claims without a named trigger event or deadline
- Success defined only as "improved" or "better" without measurement

---

## Section 2 — Key Messaging and Themes

**Purpose**: Define the 3-5 core messages the entire narrative must reinforce.
These are the throughlines that every slide, every section, every speaker note
must support.

**Required content**:
- 3-5 core messages, each stated in a single sentence
- For each message: the evidence that supports it (traced to facts manifest)
- For each message: which persona(s) it primarily addresses
- The hallway line — the single sentence someone repeats after the presentation
  (10-15 words, surprising, teaching, differentiating, repeatable)

**Quality criteria**:
- Messages are distinct (no two messages say the same thing differently)
- Each message has at least one named evidence source
- Every persona in the persona map is addressed by at least one message
- The hallway line passes the "would someone actually say this to a colleague"
  test

**Prohibited patterns**:
- More than 5 core messages (dilutes impact)
- Messages that are internally contradictory
- Generic messages that could apply to any vendor ("we deliver quality")
- A hallway line longer than 20 words

---

## Section 3 — What We Commit To

**Purpose**: State the specific, verifiable commitments. These are the
contractual promises — deliverables, SLAs, guarantees, timelines.

**Required content**:
- Specific deliverables (named outputs, not categories)
- Service level agreements with numeric targets (e.g., 99.95% uptime, <4hr
  P1 response)
- Guarantees and risk-sharing commitments (what happens if we miss targets)
- Key timeline milestones (transition dates, go-live dates, steady-state dates)

**Quality criteria**:
- Every SLA has a numeric target, not "industry-leading" or "best-in-class"
- Every deliverable is named specifically (not "comprehensive documentation")
- Timeline milestones include specific dates or durations
- Risk-sharing language is concrete (penalty clauses, service credits,
  performance bonds)

**Prohibited patterns**:
- Vague commitments ("we will ensure high availability")
- SLAs without numbers
- Timelines without dates or durations
- Promises that contradict constraints in constraints.json

---

## Section 4 — What We Know

**Purpose**: Demonstrate deep understanding of the client's environment, pain
points, and constraints. This section proves we did the homework.

**Required content**:
- Client environment description (named systems, infrastructure, team structure)
- Pain points acknowledged (specific problems the client has told us about or
  that we have observed)
- Constraints understood (budget, timeline, regulatory, political, technical)
- Lessons from any prior engagement (if applicable — reference specific outcomes)

**Quality criteria**:
- References at least 3 named client systems, teams, or processes
- Pain points are stated in the client's language (not our reframing)
- Constraints are acknowledged honestly (not minimized or ignored)
- Every claim traces to a specific source document in the facts manifest

**Prohibited patterns**:
- Generic industry observations not specific to this client
- Reframing client pain points as opportunities (acknowledge first, reframe
  later in Section 6 or 7)
- Claiming knowledge we do not have evidence for in the facts manifest
- Name-dropping client systems without demonstrating understanding of them

---

## Section 5 — How We Deliver

**Purpose**: Describe the methodology, approach, team structure, and governance
model. This is the "mechanism" that makes our commitments credible.

**Required content**:
- Delivery methodology (named framework or approach, not just "agile")
- Team structure (key roles, reporting lines, named individuals where available)
- Governance model (steering committees, escalation paths, review cadence)
- Transition approach (if applicable — how we take over from incumbent or
  current state)

**Quality criteria**:
- Methodology is specific enough to differentiate from competitors
- Team structure includes at least role titles and responsibilities
- Governance includes specific cadence (weekly, monthly, quarterly)
- Transition approach addresses the client's risk concerns

**Prohibited patterns**:
- Methodology described only by buzzword ("we use agile" without specifics)
- Team structure with no named roles
- Governance without escalation paths
- Transition plans that ignore the incumbent or current-state complexity

---

## Section 6 — How We Scale

**Purpose**: Describe the growth path, capacity model, and adaptability. How
does the solution grow with the client's needs?

**Required content**:
- Growth path (how the solution scales from initial scope to full potential)
- Capacity model (what resources scale, how quickly, at what cost increments)
- Adaptability (how the solution handles changes in requirements, technology,
  or business direction)
- Innovation roadmap (how we keep the solution current over the contract term)

**Quality criteria**:
- Growth path references specific client growth scenarios from the facts manifest
- Capacity model includes numeric thresholds (e.g., "scales to 10,000 users
  without architecture change")
- Adaptability addresses at least one specific change scenario the client has
  mentioned
- Innovation roadmap includes specific technologies or capabilities, not just
  "continuous improvement"

**Prohibited patterns**:
- Scale claims without capacity numbers
- "Future-proof" without naming what future changes are anticipated
- Innovation promises without specific investment commitments
- Growth assumptions that contradict the client's stated plans

---

## Section 7 — What the Client Gets

**Purpose**: Translate our commitments and methodology into client outcomes.
Benefits stated from the client's perspective, with measurable results.

**Required content**:
- Business outcomes (what changes for the client's business — revenue, cost,
  risk, capability)
- Measurable results (specific metrics with targets and timelines)
- User/employee impact (what changes for the people who use the systems daily)
- Strategic value (how this engagement positions the client for their next
  challenge)

**Quality criteria**:
- Every outcome has a measurement method (how will we know it worked)
- Results include both short-term (0-6 months) and long-term (12-36 months)
  targets
- User impact is described from the user's perspective, not ours
- Strategic value connects to the client's stated strategic priorities

**Prohibited patterns**:
- Benefits without measurement ("improved efficiency")
- ROI claims without showing the math
- Outcomes that only benefit us, not the client
- User impact described in technical terms the user would not use

---

## Section 8 — Why Us

**Purpose**: Close with differentiation. Why should the client choose us over
the alternatives? Proof, not assertion.

**Required content**:
- Differentiators (what we do that competitors cannot or do not — specific,
  verifiable)
- Credibility evidence (relevant experience, case studies, certifications,
  awards — named, not generic)
- Proof points (specific outcomes from similar engagements — named clients
  where permitted, anonymized with specifics where not)
- Team advantage (what is special about the specific people we are proposing)

**Quality criteria**:
- Every differentiator passes the "can a competitor say the same thing?" test
  — if yes, it is not a differentiator
- Credibility evidence is specific (named client, named project, specific
  outcome) not generic ("extensive experience in the sector")
- At least 2 proof points with measurable outcomes
- Team advantage references specific individuals or team capabilities, not
  generic "deep expertise"

**Prohibited patterns**:
- Differentiators that any competitor could claim ("experienced team",
  "proven methodology")
- Case studies without outcomes
- Claims of being "the only" provider without evidence
- Self-congratulatory language without proof ("we are passionate about...")

---

## Cross-Section Rules

These rules apply across all sections:

1. **No section may be empty**. Every section must contain at least 2 paragraphs
   of substantive content.

2. **Facts manifest traceability**. Every specific claim (named system, figure,
   date, commitment) must trace to a source in the facts manifest. If a claim
   cannot be traced, it must be flagged for verification.

3. **Persona coverage**. Across all 8 sections, every persona in the persona map
   must have at least one section that directly addresses their primary concern.
   Track this with a coverage matrix.

4. **Internal consistency**. Commitments in Section 3 must align with methodology
   in Section 5, scaling in Section 6, and outcomes in Section 7. Contradictions
   between sections are blocking issues.

5. **Tone**. Confident but not arrogant. Specific but not verbose. The exec
   summary should read as a strategic narrative, not a compliance response.
   Save the compliance-level detail for the slide content.

6. **Length**. Target 1500-3000 words total. Each section should be 150-400
   words. Longer sections suggest the content needs tightening, not expanding.
