# Brainstorm Teams — Structure and Synthesis Protocol

Reference for `deck-brainstorm` skill. Documents the three-team structure,
persona pairing, interaction protocol, and synthesis rules.

Teams and personas are **generated per deck** from the audience and topic.
This document defines the structure; the content adapts to each project.

---

## Overview

Three teams run in parallel during ideation. Each team owns a distinct angle
and produces an independent output. The outputs are synthesized — never
averaged — into a unified architecture. The tension between teams is the point:
if a claim survives all three lenses, it will survive the real audience.

---

## Team 1 — Narrative and Story

### Persona Pair

**Dreamer**: The person with the broadest vision — typically the Internal
Champion (Archetype 11) or the Impatient Sponsor (Archetype 9). Speaks in
outcomes, audience impact, and emotional arc. Tends toward aspiration.

**Skeptic**: The person who tests structure — typically the Systems Skeptic
(Archetype 8) or the Hands-on Builder (Archetype 5). Evaluates every claim
against: "What is the mechanism? What are the failure modes?"

### What This Team Produces

1. **Narrative arc** — The section structure with titles, emotional beats, and
   transitions. The arc must have a clear opening hook, a building argument,
   and a resolution.

2. **Governing principles** — The rules every slide must obey. Examples:
   - "Proof, not pitch — every claim is followed by a mechanism"
   - "Show, don't tell — evidence before assertion"
   - "The audience's problem first, our solution second"

3. **Protagonist story** — The human scenario that opens the deck and closes it.
   The deck has a protagonist. It cannot abandon the protagonist. The opening
   scenario must return in the close, transformed.

4. **Hallway line** — A 10-15 word sentence that is surprising, teaches
   immediately, differentiates, and is repeatable in conversation. Team 1
   generates 3-5 candidates; synthesis selects one.

### Team 1 Skeptic's Tests

1. "What is the mechanism?" — no mechanism = aspirational, cannot appear in deck
2. "Which specific [examples] does this apply to?" — no averages, show variance
3. "What is the failure mode?" — can't name a failure mode = don't understand it

## Team 2 — Evidence and Proof

### Persona Pair

**Dreamer**: The Domain Expert (Archetype 1) — deepest knowledge of the subject
area, can narrate the operational reality with authority.

**Skeptic**: The Hands-on Builder (Archetype 5) — lives in the detail layer.
Evaluates claims against: "Show me the artifact. Show me the actual evidence.
What does this look like in practice?"

### What This Team Produces

1. **Proof requirements** — A list of every major claim that needs evidence.
   For each: what constitutes proof, what artifact or metric backs it up.

2. **Two-layer proof pattern** — Every proof has two layers:
   - **Layer 1: Scenario** — What happens in practice? What does the person
     experience? This makes the claim feel real.
   - **Layer 2: Evidence** — The specific artifact, metric, or result. This
     makes the claim defensible.
   You cannot have one without the other. A scenario without evidence is a story.
   Evidence without scenario is a data table. Neither is a proof.

3. **Domain-specific gates** — Thresholds, criteria, or standards specific to
   the audience's world that the deck must address.

### Team 2 Skeptic's Test

One question per claim: "If I were the person living with this, what exactly
would I see?" The answer must name the specific output, metric, and decision
the evidence supports.

## Team 3 — Value Threading

### Persona Pair

**Dreamer**: The Budget Owner (Archetype 2) — sees the value proposition as
the differentiator. Understands what makes an audience commit resources.

**Skeptic**: The Procurement Gate (Archetype 3) — evaluates every value claim
against enforceability. "How is it measured? What happens if it's wrong?"

### What This Team Produces

1. **Value threading plan** — Maps the central value proposition across all
   sections of the deck using a plant-build-reveal-resolve pattern:
   - **Plant** (opening): State the value as a headline. Create the question.
   - **Build** (middle sections): Brief callbacks. Never more than 3 slides
     apart without a mention.
   - **Reveal** (dedicated section): Full explanation — how it works, how
     it's measured, what happens if it fails.
   - **Resolve** (close): Circle back to the opening. "What happens if we're
     wrong?" as the emotional peak.

2. **Measurement methodology** — For each major claim, the specific way success
   is measured. Must be specific enough that the audience can validate it.

3. **Key metrics** — For each section, the specific metric that anchors it.

4. **Discussion questions** — 2-3 questions the presenter asks at the close to
   continue the conversation. Questions must demonstrate audience knowledge.

### Team 3 Skeptic's Tests

1. "How is it measured?" — undefined measurement = disputed in year two
2. "What's the timeline?" — vague timelines kill credibility
3. "What if it fails?" — must have a failure scenario, not just success claims

## Interaction Protocol

The dreamer and skeptic work as a pair throughout, not sequentially. The
skeptic does not wait for the dreamer to finish before challenging. This is
a live negotiation, not a review.

Session structure per team:
1. **Dreamer opens** with direction (15 min)
2. **Skeptic challenges** each claim live (20 min negotiation)
3. **Pair agrees** on what survives — only surviving claims enter the output
4. **Pair documents** the output (15 min)

Teams do not observe each other. Cross-pollination happens in synthesis.
If teams influence each other during ideation, outputs converge before
synthesis, eliminating the productive tension.

## Synthesis Protocol

After all three teams complete, synthesize into a single architecture.

### Step 1 — Conflict Resolution Table

List every conflict between team outputs. For each:
- State the conflict explicitly (not "disagreement about structure" but
  "Team 1 proposed 5 sections / 20 slides; Team 3 proposed 4 sections / 25 slides")
- State the decision
- State the rationale

Resolution heuristics:

| Conflict Type | Resolution |
|---------------|-----------|
| Slide count | Adopt the structure that best supports value threading |
| Section structure | Team 1 narrative logic wins unless a break serves Team 3 culmination |
| Claim-level | Proof requirement (Team 2) wins over aspiration (Team 1) always |
| Value position | Team 3 enforceability wins over Team 1 boldness |
| Sequence | The sequence that best builds the protagonist arc (Team 1) wins |

### Step 2 — Threading Map

Map the top 3-5 key concepts to specific slide numbers. For each:
- Plant / build / reveal / resolve slides identified
- No concept has fewer than 5 slide mentions
- No gap of more than 3 consecutive slides without a mention
- Reveal slide exists for every concept

### Step 3 — Slide-by-Slide Plan

| Slide # | Title | Source | Reuse Decision | Key Edits |
|---------|-------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| 1 | [Title] | Team 1 | New | n/a |
| 2 | [Title] | Team 2 + 3 | Merge | Combine scenario with value bridge |

Reuse decisions: copy, edit, merge, new. For edits/merges, list specific changes.

### Step 4 — Production Estimate

| Category | Count |
|----------|-------|
| New build | |
| Edit from source | |
| Merge (two sources) | |
| Copy and proofread | |
| **Total** | |

### Step 5 — Approval Gate

The synthesized architecture must be approved before any slide building begins.

Approval criteria:
- All three teams signed off on conflict resolution
- Threading map verified (no gaps)
- Slide-by-slide plan complete
- Production estimate complete
- Project owner has explicitly approved

The gate: **do not begin building until this document is approved.**
