# Slide Design Principles

This document is the authoritative reference for visual design decisions in presentation slides generated by the wicked-pptx pipeline. All rules assume a 1280x720 pixel (16:9 widescreen) canvas unless stated otherwise. When working at different resolutions, scale values proportionally.

---

## Color Strategy

### Palette Construction

Limit every deck to a maximum of 4-5 colors. A well-constructed palette consists of:

- **Primary color** -- The dominant brand or theme color. Use it for backgrounds, major headings, and key accent shapes. It should carry 60-70% of the total color weight across the deck.
- **Secondary color** -- A supporting color that complements the primary. Use it for subheadings, secondary shapes, and supporting visuals. It should carry 20-25% of the color weight.
- **Accent color** -- A high-contrast color reserved for emphasis: call-to-action elements, key data points, highlighted text. Use sparingly at 5-10% of color weight. The accent must be visually distinct from both primary and secondary.
- **Neutrals** -- One or two neutral colors (white, off-white, dark gray, near-black) for body text, backgrounds, and borders. Neutrals do not count toward the 4-5 color limit when they are pure grayscale.

Do not introduce new colors on a per-slide basis. Every color on every slide must belong to the established palette. If a source HTML deck uses a color outside the palette, map it to the nearest palette color or flag it as a violation.

### Contrast Requirements (WCAG AA)

All text must meet WCAG AA contrast ratios against its immediate background:

- **Body text (below 24pt):** Minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1.
- **Large text (24pt and above, or 18.66pt bold and above):** Minimum contrast ratio of 3:1.
- **Non-text elements** (icons, borders, chart elements conveying information): Minimum contrast ratio of 3:1 against adjacent colors.

Measure contrast using the relative luminance formula defined in WCAG 2.1. When in doubt, use the higher threshold.

### Dark Background Specifics

Dark backgrounds (luminance below 0.2) require extra attention:

- Alpha-blended shapes must be evaluated at their final computed color, not the raw rgba value. A shape with `rgba(161, 0, 255, 0.06)` on a `#0D0117` background resolves to approximately `#13091D`. Check contrast against the resolved color, not the transparent input.
- White or near-white text on dark backgrounds must still meet 4.5:1 for body sizes. Pure white (#FFFFFF) on pure black (#000000) yields 21:1, which is ideal but not always aesthetically desirable. Aim for at least 7:1 when possible for comfortable reading.
- Avoid placing light-colored shapes directly on dark backgrounds without a visible border or shadow. The shape edges become ambiguous without a contrast boundary.
- When blending overlapping transparent shapes, compute the cumulative blend from back to front. The final color of the topmost visible layer is what matters for contrast.

### Color Weight Distribution

Maintain a deliberate distribution of color across each slide:

- One dominant color should cover 60-70% of the colored area (typically the background or the largest shape).
- One or two supporting colors share 20-30%.
- The accent appears in at most 5-10% of the area.

If a slide has roughly equal amounts of three or more colors, it will feel chaotic. Redesign so one color clearly dominates.

### Consistency Across Slides

The palette must remain stable across the entire deck:

- Headings use the same color on every slide.
- Body text uses the same color on every slide.
- Background color changes are acceptable only for intentional section breaks (e.g., a dark "divider" slide between light-background sections), and even then, the text and accent colors should adapt predictably.
- Do not alternate between two different accent colors from slide to slide unless there is a semantic reason (e.g., color-coded categories in a data series).

## Typography

### Font Family Limits

Use a maximum of 2 font families per deck:

- **Heading font** -- Used for slide titles, section headers, and any display text. May be a sans-serif with heavier weight or a distinctive display face.
- **Body font** -- Used for all body text, bullet points, captions, and labels. Should be a highly legible sans-serif at small sizes.

If only one font family is available (common when converting HTML to PPTX with system fonts), differentiate heading from body through weight (bold vs. regular) and size alone. Do not introduce a third font for captions or labels; use the body font at a smaller size.

### Size Hierarchy

Maintain a clear, consistent size hierarchy across every slide:

| Role | Size Range | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Slide title | 36-44pt | Bold |
| Subtitle / section header | 24-28pt | Bold or Semi-bold |
| Body text | 18-20pt | Regular |
| Caption / footnote | 12-14pt | Regular or Light |

Never set body text below 16pt. At projection sizes, anything below 16pt becomes unreadable for audience members beyond the first few rows. Never set captions below 11pt even for footnotes.

Avoid using more than 4 distinct font sizes on a single slide. If you need finer gradation, adjust weight or color instead of adding another size step.

### Line Spacing

Set line spacing (leading) between 1.2x and 1.5x the font size:

- **Headings:** 1.2x is usually sufficient since headings are short.
- **Body text:** 1.3-1.4x provides comfortable reading for multi-line paragraphs.
- **Bulleted lists:** 1.4-1.5x prevents items from running together visually.

PPTX line spacing is set via `spcBef`, `spcAft`, and `lnSpc` properties. Prefer `lnSpc` for within-paragraph spacing and `spcAft` for between-paragraph spacing.

### Alignment

- **Titles:** Center alignment is acceptable and often preferred.
- **Body text:** Left-align. Centered body text is harder to scan, especially in multi-line blocks. Never justify body text in slides; justified text creates uneven word spacing that harms readability at large sizes.
- **Numbers and data:** Right-align in tables and charts so decimal points line up.
- **Captions:** Left-align or center, consistent with the element they describe.

Do not mix left-aligned and center-aligned body text on the same slide unless they belong to structurally different regions (e.g., a centered pull quote above left-aligned bullets).

### Character Spacing

Use `charSpacing` (letter-spacing) deliberately:

- **Emphasis text and all-caps labels:** +0.5pt to +1.5pt tracking opens up uppercase text and improves legibility.
- **Body text:** Leave at default (0). Positive tracking on body text reduces reading speed.
- **Headings:** Slight positive tracking (+0.3pt to +0.8pt) can add a premium feel, but do not exceed +1.5pt or the letters disconnect visually.

## Layout

### Margins

Maintain minimum margins of 48 pixels (0.5 inches / 36pt) from all four edges of the 1280x720 canvas. This corresponds to approximately 3.75% of the slide width and 6.67% of the slide height.

- No text box, shape, or image should have its bounding box extending into the margin zone.
- If a full-bleed background image is used, overlay text must still respect the 48px margin from the image edges.
- For widescreen presentations projected on standard screens, the left and right margins are especially critical because edge content may be cropped by the projector.

### Whitespace

At least 30% of the total slide area should remain visually empty (no shapes, text, or images). This whitespace includes margins, gaps between elements, and any unfilled background area.

- Measure whitespace as the percentage of pixels on the rendered slide that show only the background color or gradient, with no foreground element overlapping.
- Slides below 30% whitespace feel cramped. When this threshold is violated, either reduce content or split across two slides.
- Whitespace is not wasted space. It directs the eye, creates breathing room, and makes the content that is present more impactful.

### Element Count

Limit each slide to a maximum of 6-7 top-level visual elements. A "top-level element" is any independently positioned shape, text box, image, or group. Child elements within a group count as one collective element.

- Slides with more than 7 elements overwhelm the viewer and dilute each element's impact.
- If a slide requires more elements (e.g., a complex diagram), group related items so the perceived count stays within bounds.
- Decorative elements (thin rules, subtle background shapes) count toward this limit. Remove decoration before adding content.

### Content Flow

Design slides to follow one of two scanning patterns:

- **Z-pattern:** For slides with a clear headline, supporting image, and concluding call-to-action. The eye moves from top-left across to top-right, diagonally down to bottom-left, then across to bottom-right.
- **F-pattern:** For text-heavy slides with headings and bullet points. The eye scans the top line fully, then drops to the next heading and scans partially, creating an F shape.

Place the most important element where the pattern begins (top-left for both patterns). Place secondary information along the natural scanning path. Place supplementary detail or attribution at the terminal point (bottom-right).

### Consistent Positioning

Slides that share a structural type (e.g., all "content" slides, all "section divider" slides) must place their common elements at the same coordinates:

- Title text boxes should have identical x, y, width, and height across all content slides.
- Body text boxes should start at the same y-coordinate and have the same width.
- Logos, slide numbers, and footer text should appear at fixed positions on every slide.

Inconsistent positioning between same-type slides creates a jarring "jumping" effect during presentations. Even a 10px vertical shift in the title between slides is noticeable.

## Visual Hierarchy

### Primary Content Placement

Place the single most important element in the upper-left quadrant of the slide. This is where the eye lands first in both Z-pattern and F-pattern scanning. The primary element is typically the slide title or a key statistic.

### One Key Message Per Slide

Every slide should communicate exactly one key message. This is the Pyramid Principle applied to presentations:

- The slide title states the key message (not a topic label, but an assertion or takeaway).
- All other content on the slide supports, explains, or illustrates that one message.
- If a slide tries to make two distinct points, split it into two slides.

Test this by covering the body content and reading only the title. If the title alone conveys the slide's purpose, the hierarchy is correct.

### Supporting Detail Placement

Supporting details (body text, bullet points, data tables, secondary charts) belong below and/or to the right of the primary content:

- Below the title for standard content slides.
- To the right of a key visual for image-and-text layouts.
- In the lower half for data-heavy slides where a chart occupies the upper portion.

### Visual Weight

Three properties create visual weight, in order of impact:

1. **Size** -- Larger elements attract attention first. The title should be the largest text on the slide.
2. **Color** -- High-saturation or high-contrast elements draw the eye. Use the accent color to highlight the most important data point or phrase.
3. **Position** -- Upper-left has the most natural weight. Lower-right has the least. Center placement implies importance but competes with the upper-left bias.

When these three properties conflict (e.g., a large element in the lower-right vs. a small accent-colored element in the upper-left), the result is visual confusion. Align size, color, and position so they reinforce the same hierarchy.

## Shapes & Containers

### Rounded Rectangles

When using rounded rectangle shapes for cards, callout boxes, or content containers:

- Set the border-radius ratio (corner radius divided by the shorter side of the rectangle) between 0.05 and 0.12.
- A ratio below 0.05 looks nearly rectangular and fails to convey the "card" metaphor.
- A ratio above 0.12 starts to look pill-shaped and wastes interior space in the corners.
- Use the same border-radius ratio for all rounded rectangles on a slide. Mixing different radii creates visual inconsistency.

### Card Shadows

For elevated card shapes that float above the background:

- **Blur radius:** 6px (gives a soft, diffused shadow edge).
- **Offset:** 2px vertical, 0-1px horizontal (light source from above).
- **Opacity:** 0.15 (15% black). Higher opacity creates harsh, outdated-looking shadows.
- **Color:** Always use black or near-black for shadows, never colored shadows.

In PPTX, shadows are controlled via the `shadow` effect on shape properties. Match these values as closely as the PPTX shadow model allows.

### Shape Style Consistency

Do not mix rounded and sharp-cornered accent shapes on the same slide or within the same deck:

- If cards use rounded rectangles, divider bars and accent shapes should also have rounded ends.
- If the design language is sharp and geometric, use squared-off rectangles throughout.
- This rule comes from Anthropic's internal research showing that mixed shape styles reduce perceived professionalism.

### Accent Bars

Thin vertical bars on the left edge of a text block or card create emphasis and visual structure:

- **Width:** 2-4pt (thinner bars feel more refined; thicker bars feel more assertive).
- **Height:** Match the height of the text block or card they accent.
- **Color:** Use the primary or accent color from the palette.
- **Position:** Flush with the left edge of the associated element, with 8-12px gap between the bar and the text.

Accent bars are particularly effective for distinguishing quoted text, key takeaways, or sidebar content from the main body. Do not use them on every text block; reserve them for emphasis.
